The same pipeline scores 0.275 on QASPER and 0.672 on LitSearch (Recall@10). Each rung below adds exactly one component, so each jump is attributable to one design decision — QASPER's last two rungs came from measuring the retrieval ceiling first and fixing the stage that was actually binding. On LitSearch, the full stack lands within 2 points of GritLM-7B, the strongest retriever the benchmark's authors tested — with nothing bigger than 0.6 B parameters.

Pipeline. Dense retrieval (BGE-M3) + lexical BM25, fused with Reciprocal Rank Fusion, then re-ranked by a BGE cross-encoder. Both benchmarks scored against human relevance labels — retrieval evaluated before any answer generation. +enrich indexes each QASPER paragraph as title + section + text, so under-specified questions can find chunks by document identity; +deep rerank widens the cross-encoder pool from 50 to 100 fused candidates.
Deep reranking was measured on LitSearch too and rejected: 0.667 vs 0.672 Recall@10 — a deeper pool pays only when the reranker out-discriminates the added distractors. LitSearch therefore keeps depth 50. Bars use per-panel axes (labelled) so each ablation reads clearly; the cross-benchmark gap is in the numbers, not the bar heights. Published baselines: Ajith et al., LitSearch (EMNLP 2024), arXiv:2407.18940. QASPER has no comparable public leaderboard for this framing — the standard task retrieves within a single paper; here the corpus is all 20,221 paragraphs across 416 papers at once.
